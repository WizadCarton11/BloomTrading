A stock represents ownership in a company. When you own stock in a company, you become a shareholder, meaning you have a claim on a portion of the company’s assets and earnings.

Stocks are also referred to as equities because they represent a form of equity ownership.

Companies issue stocks to raise capital for expansion, research, and other business activities.

A share is a single unit of stock.

When a company issues stock, it is divided into a certain number of shares, and each share represents a fractional ownership in the company.

For example, if a company has issued 1 million shares and you own 1,000 shares, you own 0.1% of the company.

Equity refers to the ownership interest in a company, represented by stocks.

It is the difference between a company’s total assets and total liabilities.

Investors buy equity (stocks) in the hope that the company will grow and generate profits, which will increase the stock’s value.

A bond is a fixed-income investment in which an investor loans money to an entity (corporate or government) for a fixed period at a specified interest rate.

The borrower (issuer) agrees to pay back the principal amount on a specific maturity date, along with periodic interest payments (coupon).

Bonds are considered less risky than stocks but offer lower returns.

A dividend is a portion of a company’s earnings paid to shareholders, usually in cash or additional shares.

Companies that generate steady profits often distribute dividends to reward their shareholders.

Not all stocks pay dividends—some companies reinvest profits for growth instead of distributing them.

Market Capitalization (or Market Cap) is the total value of a company’s outstanding shares.

Companies are often categorized based on their market cap:

Large-cap (>$10 billion) – Stable, well-established companies.

Mid-cap ($2 billion - $10 billion) – Companies with growth potential.

Small-cap (<$2 billion) – Riskier but high-growth potential.


An IPO is when a private company offers its shares to the public for the first time.

Before an IPO, a company is privately owned, usually by founders, venture capitalists, or early investors.

After an IPO, the company’s shares trade on a stock exchange, allowing the general public to invest.

Blue-chip stocks are shares of large, well-established, financially stable companies with a history of steady growth and reliable dividends.

These companies usually have strong brand recognition and operate in stable industries.

Examples: Apple, Microsoft, Coca-Cola, and Johnson & Johnson.

Blue-chip stocks are considered lower risk compared to smaller companies and are often included in long-term investment portfolios.

Penny stocks are shares of small, lesser-known companies that trade at a very low price, typically below $5 per share.

They are highly speculative and risky due to low liquidity, high volatility, and lack of financial transparency.

Since these stocks belong to smaller companies, they have the potential for massive gains but also a high risk of failure.

They often trade on over-the-counter (OTC) markets rather than major exchanges like the NYSE or Nasdaq.


Common stock represents ownership in a company and grants voting rights to shareholders.

Shareholders may receive dividends, but these are not guaranteed.

In case of bankruptcy, common stockholders are the last to receive payouts, after bondholders and preferred shareholders.

It offers the potential for capital appreciation (increase in stock price) but comes with higher risk.


Preferred stock is a special type of stock that has priority over common stock in receiving dividends and assets during liquidation.

Unlike common stock, preferred shareholders do not have voting rights in most cases.

They receive fixed dividends, making it similar to a bond.

Companies issue preferred stock to raise capital while avoiding dilution of common shareholders' voting power.

A stock market index is a benchmark that tracks the performance of a group of stocks, representing a portion of the market.

It helps investors gauge market trends and compare stock performance.

Major indices include:

S&P 500 – Tracks the 500 largest publicly traded U.S. companies.

Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) – Tracks 30 major U.S. companies.

NASDAQ Composite – Includes all stocks listed on the Nasdaq exchange, with a heavy focus on technology stocks.

Investors use indices to measure overall market health and as a basis for index funds or ETFs.

An ETF is a fund that holds a collection of stocks, bonds, or commodities but trades like a stock on an exchange.

ETFs offer diversification by investing in multiple assets, reducing risk.

They have lower fees compared to mutual funds because they are passively managed.

Examples:

SPY (S&P 500 ETF) – Tracks the S&P 500 index.

QQQ (Nasdaq-100 ETF) – Tracks the top 100 tech stocks.

ETFs can be bought and sold throughout the trading day at market prices, just like stocks.

A mutual fund is a professionally managed investment pool that collects money from multiple investors to invest in a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other assets.

Unlike ETFs, mutual funds are actively managed, meaning fund managers make decisions to try to outperform the market.

They are bought and sold at the end of the trading day at the fund's Net Asset Value (NAV).

Mutual funds are ideal for long-term investors looking for professional management and diversification.

A Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) is a company that owns, operates, or finances income-generating real estate.

REITs allow investors to invest in real estate without directly owning property.

They pay high dividends because they are required to distribute at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders.

Types of REITs:

Equity REITs – Own and manage properties like apartments, malls, and office buildings.

Mortgage REITs (mREITs) – Invest in real estate loans and mortgages.

Hybrid REITs – A mix of equity and mortgage REITs.

REITs trade on major stock exchanges and provide a way to gain exposure to the real estate market.
